# Voice & Tone

## The one-line summary

**Confident but accessible. Smart but not cold.**

Every example below is real, production copy already sent to real candidates — this isn't
an aspirational voice guide, it's a description of how CBT already writes when it's writing
well. Use these as templates for new communications rather than starting from a blank page.

## Core principles

1. **Address the person, not "the applicant."** Every email opens with a name, not a
   generic "Dear Sir/Madam." Even system-generated messages read as if a person wrote them.
2. **Lead with the outcome, then explain.** Good news or bad, the first sentence says what
   happened. Instructions and details follow — never bury the headline in a preamble.
3. **Be specific about next steps.** Vague emails create support burden. CBT's copy always
   tells the reader exactly what to do next, often as a numbered list.
4. **Stay warm even in rejection.** A "no" is still delivered with respect and an explicit
   invitation to try again — see the rejection example below.
5. **Sign off as a team, not a machine.** Every email closes as "Recruitment Team,
   Convergent Business Technologies" — never "Automated System" or "No-Reply."

## Annotated examples

### Good news — assessment invite

> Congratulations, {name}!
>
> Your application for the CGAP program has been approved. The next step is a technical
> assessment.
>
> Please use the link below to select a convenient time slot for your assessment. You can
> also use this same link to reschedule your assessment if needed.
>
> If you have any questions, feel free to reply to this email.

**Why this works:** Opens with genuine congratulations, not a status code. States the next
step in one sentence. Proactively answers the "what if I need to change it?" question before
the candidate has to ask. Ends by inviting a reply — never a dead-end no-reply address.

### Good news — final selection (with logistics)

> Dear {name},
>
> On behalf of Convergent Business Technologies, we are delighted to inform you that you
> have been **selected** for the **Convergent Graduate Academy Program (CGAP)**.
> Congratulations on your selection.
>
> The CGAP will commence with an orientation session. Details regarding the orientation will
> be shared with you separately via email. Please note that you will be required to bring
> your **original CNIC** with you on the day of the orientation session for verification
> purposes.
>
> To complete your enrolment process, please follow the steps below...
>
> *(numbered onboarding steps, then required documents, then a clearly highlighted deadline
> callout box)*
>
> Should you have any questions or require any assistance, feel free to reach out before the
> deadline.
>
> We look forward to welcoming you to the CGAP.

**Why this works:** Formal enough for what is effectively a legal/administrative process
(PSEB enrolment, document submission), but every paragraph is still addressed to a person,
not "all candidates." The deadline is visually called out (red left-border box) rather than
buried in a paragraph — critical information gets critical visual treatment. Closes with
genuine enthusiasm ("we look forward to welcoming you"), not just a procedural sign-off.

### Bad news — not recommended

> Hello {name},
>
> I am writing to you as a follow-up to your application for the Convergent Graduate Academy
> Program.
>
> Thank you for your interest in our program and for taking the time to apply. After careful
> review of your application, we regret to inform you that you have not been shortlisted to
> proceed further in the recruitment process.
>
> However, we strongly encourage you to apply again after six (06) months.
>
> We appreciate your interest in Convergent and wish you the very best in your future
> endeavors.

**Why this works:** This is the hardest tone to get right, and CBT's version threads the
needle well. It doesn't over-apologize or grovel, but it also doesn't hide behind passive
corporate-speak. "We regret to inform you" is one clear, respectful sentence — not three
paragraphs of cushioning. The explicit reapplication window turns a closed door into a
half-open one. It closes by genuinely wishing them well, not just "regards."

### Instructional — assessment day logistics

> Hello!
>
> I am writing to you as a follow-up to your application to the Convergent Graduate Academy
> Program. It is to inform you that you have been short-listed for the initial assessment.
>
> **Assessment Date/Time:** {slot time}
> *(Followed by an interview after 1:00 PM if the threshold score is achieved)*
>
> The Assessment Test will be computer-based and consist of multiple-choice questions
> covering the following topics: Reading Comprehension, Analytical Thinking, Data
> Structures, SQL, Python Programming.
>
> **Please take note of the following instructions:**
> - Please make sure to bring your CNIC
> - During the assessment, make sure to turn off your mobile device. Using any such device
>   during the assessment will result in immediate disqualification
> - Revise the core concepts thoroughly prior to the assessment
> - Read through all instructions before attempting the test
> - Focus on addressing each question individually
>
> **Address:** Convergent Business Technologies, Fourth floor, Plot. No. 64, Civic Center,
> Executive Block, Gulberg Greens, Islamabad. [View on Google Maps →]
>
> *Note: When you reach the premises, please feel free to inform the guard of your arrival,
> or you can contact us on: Office Admin: +92 342 937 0603, Landline: (051) 591 2926*
>
> Thank you for your attention to these details, and we look forward to your participation.

**Why this works:** Logistics-heavy emails are where brands usually go cold and robotic —
this one doesn't. The disqualification rule is stated firmly and clearly (no ambiguity about
something that matters), but it's surrounded by warmth: an offer to inform the guard on
arrival, real phone numbers for a real person, and a genuine closing sentiment.

## Quick checklist for any new CBT-branded copy

- [ ] Does it open by naming the person, not their application/case?
- [ ] Does the first sentence say what happened, before any preamble?
- [ ] Are next steps concrete and numbered/bulleted where there's more than one?
- [ ] If it's bad news, is there still a path forward stated explicitly?
- [ ] Does it close as "Recruitment Team, Convergent Business Technologies," signed as a
      team — not an automated system?
- [ ] Would a real recruiter reading this out loud sound like a caring, competent person —
      not a script?
